CAREER PROGRESSION: More Than Just Job Titles 📈
Your journey through previous roles can speak volumes about your potential. Here’s how to effectively present this progression:

Show Growth 🌱

Illustrate your career trajectory by not only listing your titles but also explaining the advancements or level changes within the company. This demonstrates ambition, resilience, and the ability to adapt and grow within an organization. For more detailed tips on effectively presenting your career trajectory on your resume, consider reading the insightful article: What’s the Best Way to Show Career Progression on a Resume?

Highlight Relevant Experiences 🎯

For each position, use bullets to describe your responsibilities and the tangible outcomes of your work. Focus on achievements that align with the requirements of the job you are applying for.

KEYWORDS ARE LESS RELEVANT THAN YOU THINK
While it’s important to include keywords from the job description, stuffing your resume with them can do more harm than good. Here’s a better approach:

Natural Integration 🌿

Incorporate keywords naturally into your resume. Align them with your skills and experiences in a way that makes sense contextually, rather than simply listing them. To get more insights on how to strategically use keywords in your resume, you can explore this article: How to Use a Job Description Keywords Finder for Your Resume.

ENSURE YOUR TITLE IS COMMON: Speak Their Language 🗣️
Job titles vary widely across industries and companies. Here’s how to make yours clear:

Use Standard Job Titles 🏷️

If your internal job title is unique or customized (like “Technical Staff Engineer”), align it with more commonly recognized titles (like “Software Engineer”) that recruiters are likely to understand and search for.


DEEPLY UNDERSTAND THE COMPANY: Tailor Your Resume 🎯

Research Thoroughly 🔍

Use platforms like LinkedIn, the company’s press page, and Crunchbase to gather insights about the company’s culture, goals, and recent achievements. Reflect your understanding and alignment with these aspects throughout your resume.


Customize Accordingly 👔

Adapt your resume to echo the company’s values and the specifics of the job role. This personalized approach shows genuine interest and initiative, traits highly valued by employers.

Your Resume and ATS 🎯

Optimizing your resume for ATS is not about tricking the system but about presenting your professional narrative in a clear, concise, and relevant manner. By focusing on value, career progression, appropriate keyword use, clear titling, and company-specific tailoring, you can create a resume that not only passes through ATS but also resonates with human recruiters.

### Purpose

This page captures the 2026 resume expectations for a modern **data engineer** (increasingly a *software engineer specialized in data*) and translates them into *what to show on a resume*.

---

### Executive summary (2026 signal)

In 2026, strong resumes show:

- **Software craftsmanship**, not just Python syntax.
- **Platform thinking**: reliability, testing, CI/CD, and operational maturity.
- **AI readiness** through *data platform robustness* (semantic layer, governance, workload isolation), not “random AI tools.”
- **Architecture + FinOps**: defend trade-offs, cost awareness, and open table formats.
- **Orchestration + data quality** as non-negotiables (contracts, observability, guardrails).

---

### Phase 1: Software Craftsmanship Over Syntax (1:12–2:59)

#### What the speaker is saying

A 2026 data engineer is a **software engineer for data**. The bar is writing **robust, modular, testable code** that survives:

- Cloud outages
- Schema changes
- Bad API responses

…without late-night firefighting.

Key roadmap items:

- **Testing** (e.g., Pytest)
- **CI/CD** (e.g., GitHub Actions, Databricks Asset Bundles)
- **Containerization** (Docker)

Anecdote: lack of testing caused **silent production errors**, missed files, incorrect data, failed audits, and major business cost.

#### What I should show on my resume

**Resume bullets should prove craftsmanship with evidence**:

- Wrote modular Python packages for pipelines (clear interfaces, reusable components).
- Added unit tests and prevented regressions (quantify reduction in incidents).
- Built CI/CD for data code (deployments, validations, automated tests).
- Containerized pipeline runtime to ensure dev-to-prod consistency.
- Designed resilience for API failures and schema drift.

#### Keywords that read as “real engineering”

- Unit tests, integration tests, test fixtures, mocking
- CI/CD, build pipelines, deployment pipelines
- Docker images, reproducible environments
- Error handling, retries, idempotency, backoff, dead-letter queues (if applicable)
- Incident reduction, audit readiness, change management

#### Example bullet templates (fill in with your specifics)

- “Built Pytest test suite + CI pipeline (GitHub Actions) for X pipelines, reducing production data defects by Y% and cutting on-call pages by Z.”
- “Refactored a monolithic ingestion job into modular components and added contract tests to handle schema drift with zero manual intervention.”

---

### Phase 2: AI Infrastructure & Semantic Layers (3:00–5:41)

#### What the speaker is saying

AI projects fail mostly due to **engineering** (not math). The data engineer is building the **plumbing** that keeps AI alive:

- Handle **schema drift** without breaking models.
- **Isolate heavy compute** (training) from latency-sensitive analytics.
- Build/own the **semantic layer** so metrics (e.g., LTV) are consistent across:
    - Executive dashboards
    - AI agents
    - Finance stakeholders
- Expose metrics to LLMs without forcing complex SQL.

Cautionary tale: business logic hidden in reporting tools caused an AI initiative to fail because the agent could not understand core metrics.

#### What I should show on my resume

Focus on **platform outcomes**, not “played with LLM tools.”

- Implemented a governed semantic layer / metrics definitions (single source of truth).
- Standardized business logic in the warehouse/lakehouse.
- Segmented workloads (resource isolation) to protect production reporting.
- Improved time-to-ship for DS/ML by improving data contracts, datasets, and reliability.

#### Keywords that matter

- Semantic layer, metrics layer, governed metrics, definitions
- Workload isolation, resource queues, compute separation
- Schema drift handling, backward compatibility, versioning
- “LLM-ready metrics” (exposing curated metrics endpoints), retrieval-ready datasets

#### Example bullet templates

- “Created governed metric definitions for X KPIs used by Finance + ML pipelines, reducing metric discrepancies from N to 0 across dashboards and models.”
- “Isolated model-training workloads from BI queries using separate compute + priority policies, improving dashboard latency by X%.”

---

### Phase 3: Architecture & Governance (5:44–8:30)

#### What the speaker is saying

Cloud cost is now under audit. Storage is cheap, **compute is expensive**.

If you list Spark but cannot explain:

- partition pruning
- shuffle operations
- broadcast joins

…you risk being a liability.

Roadmap:

- Table formats like **Iceberg** and **Delta Lake**
- **Open standards** (avoid lock-in)
- **FinOps**: design for **total cost of ownership**

Example: streaming architecture costing $15k/month for a use case that only needed daily updates.

Message: **Strategy beats syntax**.

#### What I should show on my resume

- Architecture decisions tied to business needs (latency, freshness, cost).
- Concrete cost/performance wins.
- Governance: access controls, lineage, documentation, standards.
- Deep Spark performance literacy *if you claim Spark*.

#### Keywords that matter

- FinOps, cost optimization, TCO
- Partitioning, pruning, file formats (Parquet), compaction
- Shuffle reduction, broadcast joins, caching
- Delta Lake / Iceberg, open table formats
- Governance, RBAC, auditability

#### Example bullet templates

- “Reduced warehouse compute cost by X% by rewriting joins, adding partitioning, and optimizing file layout (compaction + clustering).”
- “Designed batch-first architecture for daily SLA use case, avoiding unnecessary streaming spend and saving ~$X/month.”

---

### Phase 4: Orchestration & Data Quality (8:33–10:01)

#### What the speaker is saying

Cron jobs are not acceptable for serious platforms. Engineers need orchestration that supports:

- Dependencies
- Retries
- Backfills

Master one orchestration tool:

- Airflow, Dagster, or Databricks Jobs

Most important: **data quality is no longer optional**. Bad data can trigger automated AI actions with real consequences.

Roadmap:

- Data contracts
- Observability
- Schema enforcement (e.g., Monte Carlo, Great Expectations)
- Stop bad data at the source

#### What I should show on my resume

- Production orchestration ownership (not hobby DAGs).
- Backfill strategy + idempotency + SLAs.
- Data quality checks and incident prevention.
- Monitoring: metrics, alerts, lineage, freshness, volume, null rates.

#### Keywords that matter

- Orchestration, DAGs, dependency management, backfills
- SLAs/SLOs, on-call readiness
- Data contracts, schema enforcement
- Data observability, anomaly detection
- Great Expectations / Monte Carlo (or equivalent)

#### Example bullet templates

- “Implemented automated data validation suite (schema + freshness + volume), preventing X classes of incidents and improving trust in executive metrics.”
- “Built backfill-safe pipelines with idempotent loads and automated retry policies, cutting recovery time from X hours to Y.”

- [ ]  I have at least 2–4 bullets that demonstrate **testing + CI/CD**.
- [ ]  I have at least 2 bullets that show **reliability** (resilience, schema drift, incident reduction).
- [ ]  I have at least 1–2 bullets that show **architecture trade-offs** with cost reasoning.
- [ ]  If I list Spark, I can defend performance concepts with examples.
- [ ]  I show orchestration ownership (Airflow/Dagster/Databricks Jobs) in production context.
- [ ]  I show data quality and observability implementation, not just “care about quality.”
- [ ]  I show semantic layer / metrics consistency work (especially important for AI readiness).

#### Red flags to remove

- Vague claims (“optimized pipelines”, “improved performance”) without metrics.
- Listing many tools without a story of impact.
- AI buzzwords without platform fundamentals (quality, governance, cost, reliability).
